Raspberry Pi 400

Source:Abdulla Al Muhairi, Raspberry Pi 400 kit, en.wikipedia.org, (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raspberry_Pi#/media/File:Raspberry_Pi_400_(50586757772).jpg)

The Raspberry Pi 400 takes a different approach to tiny computers than the other Pi’s did. Unlike the single exposed board sort of presentation of the previous versions, the Pi 400 is stored within a keyboard. Also unlike its predecessors, the Pi 400 costs $70, double the usual $35 of a Raspberry Pi board. The Raspberry Pi Foundation released this product after the beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic, where they saw the rise of their previous product, the Raspberry Pi 4, being used for working at home and studying too. The Foundation believed that a less complicated design with less clutter would provide a more friendly user experience, and form such an idea the Raspberry Pi 400 was born. The Pi 400 holds within its keyboard casing 4 gigabytes of RAM, much more than the majority of other Pi boards, as well as the capability of 4k video, wireless internet, displaying information on more than one screen, and a 64-bit quad-core processor. These features effectively allowed the system to be a Raspberry Pi 4 but faster, cleaner, and more consumer-friendly. This user-friendly mission is reflected in a simple pack offered concerning this product; $100 for the system, a power supply, an SD card with the OS pre-installed, a mouse, a guide to the Raspberry Pi catered towards beginners, and an HDMI cable. All of this makes for an excellently simple gift for a loved one interested in improving their workstation setup without spending a metric ton of cash on a fancy newfangled desktop or laptop. Plus, it’s much easier to move a keyboard and mouse around than an entire laptop, and you can do just that with the Raspberry Pi 400 so long as you can find a screen to utilize.
